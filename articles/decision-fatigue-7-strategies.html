Decision Fatigue: 7 Strategies to Save Your Mental Energy for What Matters

Published: May 20, 2026 | 8 min read | Category: Energy Management

You make roughly 35,000 decisions per day. What to wear, what to eat, which email to answer first, which task to prioritize, whether to respond to that Slack message now or later. Each decision depletes a finite reservoir of mental energy. By the end of the day, your ability to make good decisions is significantly lower than it was in the morning. This is decision fatigue.

Research by Roy Baumeister and others shows that decision-making depletes the same mental resource as self-control. When that resource is low, you make worse decisions, procrastinate more, and have less willpower. Here are 7 strategies to conserve your decision-making energy.

1. Automate Your Morning Routine

Steve Jobs wore the same black turtleneck every day. Barack Obama wore only gray or blue suits. They understood that trivial decisions what to wear, what to eat for breakfast drain the same energy you need for important decisions. Create a morning routine so automatic you don't have to think about it. Same wake-up time, same breakfast, same pre-work ritual. Save your decision energy for creative work.

2. Use the "Two-List" System

You can't prioritize 20 things. Write a list of everything you could do today or this week. Then draw a line after the top 3. Everything below the line is explicitly not your priority right now. This isn't a "maybe later" list it's a "not now" list. The act of deciding what NOT to do is more important than deciding what to do.

3. Batch Similar Decisions

Schedule all your decisions of the same type at the same time. Batch email into two daily blocks. Batch meal planning into Sunday afternoon. Batch financial decisions into the first weekend of the month. When you batch, you make many decisions in one focused session, leaving the rest of your week decision-light.

4. Create Decision Templates

Create reusable templates for recurring decisions: a weekly meal plan template, a workout schedule template, a weekly planning template, a packing list for travel. When the template is already set, you don't need to decide each element you just execute the template.

5. Implement the 80/20 Rule for Decisions

Most decisions don't require exhaustive analysis. For 80% of your decisions, the best option is good enough. Perfectionism in decision-making researching every option, weighing every trade-off is a major source of decision fatigue. Use a simple heuristic: if the decision can't significantly impact your life, make it in under 60 seconds.

6. Reduce Your Number of Inputs

Each input channel email inbox, Slack channels, social media feeds, news apps presents you with a stream of micro-decisions. Should I read this? Should I respond? Should I act? Each micro-decision chips away at your decision energy. Reduce your inputs. Unsubscribe from email lists. Leave Slack channels. Turn off news notifications.

7. Schedule Important Decisions for Morning

Your decision-making ability peaks in the first 2-3 hours after waking. Schedule your most important decisions for this window. Avoid making significant decisions in the late afternoon or evening when decision fatigue is highest. If a big decision lands at 4 PM, sleep on it and decide in the morning.

The Ego Depletion Effect: Studies show that judges are far more likely to grant parole early in the day or after a food break. The same judge who approved parole at 9 AM denies it at 4 PM not because the cases are different, but because the judge's decision-making energy is depleted. The same effect applies to your decisions. Protect your morning decision energy.

Decision fatigue is invisible but costly. Each trivial decision you make throughout the day is a small withdrawal from your mental energy account. By automating, batching, and simplifying decisions, you conserve that energy for the decisions that actually matter the ones that move your Life OS forward.
