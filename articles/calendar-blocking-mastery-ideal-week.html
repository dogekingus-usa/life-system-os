Step 2: Theme Your Days

Task switching costs 23 minutes per switch. Theming your days eliminates most switching by grouping similar activities. Here's a sample theme structure:

  • Monday: Deep Work â€” No meetings. Two 90-minute blocks for your biggest project.
  • Tuesday: Internal â€” Team meetings, 1:1s, collaboration.

Day theming creates strong psychological containers. Your brain knows "Tuesday is for meetings" and doesn't fight it. Your brain also knows "Monday is for deep work" and settles into focus more quickly.

Pro Tip: If your weeks vary wildly, theme by time of day instead. Mornings = deep work (every day). Afternoons = meetings and admin (every day). Consistency of time beats consistency of day.

Step 3: Block for Energy, Not Just Time

Time is uniform â€” one hour is the same length whether it's 8 AM or 8 PM. Your energy is not. Block your calendar according to your natural energy curve:

  • Peak energy (first 3 hours after waking): Deep work blocks only. Your best cognitive work belongs here.
  • Mid-energy (next 2-3 hours): Meetings, collaboration, strategic thinking. Still productive but less intense.
  • Low energy (afternoon slump): Admin, email, routine tasks, learning. Low cognitive demand work.
  • Second wind (late afternoon): Creative work, planning, exercise. Many people have a second, smaller peak here.

Step 4: Build Buffer Blocks

The most common reason calendar blocking fails: unexpected things happen. A meeting runs long. An urgent issue appears. You underestimate a task. Buffer blocks absorb these shocks without destroying your schedule.

Build at least two buffer blocks per day â€” one in the morning (30 min) and one in the afternoon (30 min). These are unscheduled time for overflow, emergencies, and spontaneous opportunities. If you don't use them, you get unexpected free time â€” a gift.

Common Calendar Blocking Pitfalls

Pitfall 1: Over-scheduling. Don't fill every minute. Leave 20-30% of your calendar unscheduled for flexibility. A fully booked calendar has no room for reality.

Pitfall 2: Ignoring recovery. Your brain needs breaks. Block them with the same seriousness as client meetings. Lunch is not optional â€” it's a performance requirement.

Pitfall 3: Letting meetings invade deep work blocks. When someone schedules a meeting during your deep work block, you have two choices: move the meeting or protect your block. Protect your block. Your deep work is more valuable than 95% of meetings.

Pitfall 4: Not reviewing. Your ideal week template is a hypothesis, not a law. Each week, review what worked and adjust. After 4 weeks, you'll have a calendar design that's personalized to your rhythms.
